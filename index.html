Gestures in speech and social interaction

A multimodal essay

B118498

 

Humans do not only communicate using language; gesture is just as universal to us as a species, and gesture and language go hand in hand (Abner et al., 2015, p. 437). Despite this, gestures are often omitted from discussions about communication (Abner et al., 2015, p. 437). In this essay, I will provide an overview of different types of gestures used in speech and social interaction. I will illustrate the differences between different types of gestures with the help of interactive examples that will show how each type is distinct from the others.

As the title states, this is a multimodal essay, but what exactly does this mean? Furthermore, what is meant by the term gesture in the first place? Before we discuss different types of gestures, it is important to first establish what is meant by these two key concepts.

 

What is multimodality?

(click me)

According to Jewitt et al. (2016), multimodality questions the idea that there is a strict division of labour between different means of meaning making and instead highlights the fact that these different means almost always appear together: for example, image with writing, and speech with gesture (p. 2). These means for making meaning are often called modalities or modes (Jewitt et al., 2016, p. 2).

Jewitt et al. (2016) also listed three key premises of multimodality:

  1. Meaning is made with different semiotic resources, each offering distinct potentialities and limitations.
  2. Meaning making involves the production of multimodal whiles.
  3. If we want to study meaning, we need to attend to all semiotic resources being used to make a complete whole. (p. 3)

What are gestures?

(click me)

Kendon (2004) defined gestures as “visible action when it is used as an utterance or as part of an utterance.” According to Abner et al. (2015), these visible actions include not only points, shrugs, and nods, but also illustrations of the size, shape, and location of objects, demonstrations of how to perform actions, and depictions of abstract ideas and relationships (p. 437).

Gestures, like speech, can be segmented. Abner et al. (2015) described the phases of gestures as follows:

The preparation phase is the movement of the hand as it readies itself for the gestural stroke. The stroke phase is the most effortful and most meaningful phase of the gesture. It may then be followed by a retraction phase, where the hand returns to resting position, or it may be followed by the preparation or stroke phase of a subsequent gesture. (p. 438)

Here, the gestures have been labelled 1. interactive and 2. representational. This is because one way of classifying gestures is according to their function in communication (Abner et al., 2015, p. 438). The former label is used for gestures that manage the dialogue between interlocutors and help frame the speech within its discourse context — for example when indicating that an idea, a proposal, or an observation is being presented (Abner et al., 2015, pp. 438-439). This is illustrated by the upheld palm in (1). The latter term, on the other hand, is used for gestures that communicate something about the topic or primary content of the utterance (Abner et al., 2015, p. 438). This can be, for example, pointing to objects or locations as in (2).

The gestures that have just been illustrated are, as one can imagine, only two examples of the vast number of gestures that speakers and hearers use when communicating. In fact, representational gestures can be further split up into categories that describe how they "mean" in different ways (Abner et al., 2015, p. 439).
