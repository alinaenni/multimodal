Gestures in speech and social interaction

A multimodal essay

B118498

 

Humans do not only communicate using language; gesture is just as universal to us as a species, and gesture and language go hand in hand (Abner et al., 2015, p. 437). Despite this, gestures are often omitted from discussions about communication (Abner et al., 2015, p. 437). In this essay, I will delve into the world of gestures and discuss one way of classifying them in detail. I will illustrate the differences between different types of gestures with the help of interactive examples that will show how each type is distinct from the others.

As the title states, this is a multimodal essay, but what exactly does this mean? Furthermore, what is meant by the term gesture in the first place? Before we discuss different types of gestures, it is important to first establish what is meant by these two key concepts.

 

What is multimodality?

(click me)

According to Jewitt et al. (2016), multimodality questions the idea that there is a strict division of labour between different means of meaning making and instead highlights the fact that these different means almost always appear together: for example, image with writing, and speech with gesture (p. 2). These means for making meaning are often called modalities or modes (Jewitt et al., 2016, p. 2).

Jewitt et al. (2016) also listed three key premises of multimodality:

  1. Meaning is made with different semiotic resources, each offering distinct potentialities and limitations.
  2. Meaning making involves the production of multimodal wholes.
  3. If we want to study meaning, we need to attend to all semiotic resources being used to make a complete whole. (p. 3)

What are gestures?

(click me)

Kendon (2004) defined gestures as “visible action when it is used as an utterance or as part of an utterance.” According to Abner et al. (2015), these visible actions include not only points, shrugs, and nods, but also illustrations of the size, shape, and location of objects, demonstrations of how to perform actions, and depictions of abstract ideas and relationships (p. 437).

Gestures, like speech, can be segmented. Abner et al. (2015) described the phases of gestures as follows:

The preparation phase is the movement of the hand as it readies itself for the gestural stroke. The stroke phase is the most effortful and most meaningful phase of the gesture. It may then be followed by a retraction phase, where the hand returns to resting position, or it may be followed by the preparation or stroke phase of a subsequent gesture. (p. 438)

Here, the gestures have been labelled 1. interactive and 2. representational. This is because one way of classifying gestures is according to their function in communication (Abner et al., 2015, p. 438). The former label is used for gestures that manage the dialogue between interlocutors and help frame the speech within its discourse context — for example when indicating that an idea, a proposal, or an observation is being presented (Abner et al., 2015, pp. 438-439). This is illustrated by the upheld palm in (1). The latter term, on the other hand, is used for gestures that communicate something about the topic or primary content of the utterance (Abner et al., 2015, p. 438). This can be, for example, pointing to objects or locations as in (2).

The gestures that have just been illustrated are, as one can imagine, only two examples of the vast number of gestures that speakers and hearers use when communicating. In fact, representational gestures can be further split up into categories that describe how they 'mean' in different ways (Abner et al., 2015, p. 439). In the following section, four different types of representational gestures will be illustrated.

Types of representational gestures

Deictic gesture

Deictic gestures ‘mean’ directly by pointing to objects or locations in space (Abner et al., 2015, p. 439). The earlier image used to illustrate a representational gesture (2) was also an example of a deictic gesture. Both of these examples show a person holding out their index finger to draw their interlocutors’ attention to something that is relevant to the discourse.

However, this motion by no means the only way to point. Speakers of Arrernte, an Australian Aboriginal language, distinguish as many as six different pointing gestures — for instance, open-hand pointing, ‘horn-hand’ pointing, lip pointing, and eye pointing (Kita, 2009, p. 148).

Iconic gesture

Gestures that depict concrete imagery are often called iconic (Abner et al., 2015, p. 439). In this case, the person gesturing is using both of their hands, both palms down, seemingly to illustrate the physical size of an object.

This kind of gesture can add detail to a story the speaker is telling and will subsequently help a hearer to construct a mental image of the object under discussiong that matches that of the speaker’s. Another example of an iconic gesture would be a demonstration of an action such as swinging or hitting, illustrated with a swift hand movement, for example.

 

Metaphoric gesture

Metaphoric gestures are similar to iconic gestures in that they depict imagery, but instead of illustrating the properties of something concrete or physical, they depict abstract concepts (Abner et al., 2015, p. 439). In this case, the person gesturing is linking the tips of their thumbs and the rest of their fingers to create the shape of a heart, seemingly to represent the concept of love or affection.

Beattie and Sale (2012) investigated the influence of metaphoric gestures on hearer interpretations and found that the interpretation of an utterance is affected by a gesture-speech mismatch, showing that hearers extract meaning from abstract metaphoric gestures.

Emblem

A gesture can have a meaning due to a convention in a community, and these conventionalised gestures are called emblems (Abner et al., 2015, p. 439). The meaning is conventionalised in the same way that a given word has its unique meaning in a language — such as ‘flower’ in English.

In this case, the person gesturing is illustrating the emblem that conventionally signifies a wish or hopefulness. This gesture is created by crossing the index and middle fingers and holding them up. Other recognisable emblems include the thumbs up for 'good' or 'positive' and the circling index finger for 'crazy' (Abner et al., 2015, p. 439).

Is this all there is to gestures?

Of course not!

As has been shown so far, gestures can be classified according to their function in communication. However, this is only one of the ways gestures have been categorised by researchers. McNeill (2008) referred to what he has termed Kendon’s continuum which consists of gesticulation, emblems, pantomime, and sign language (p. 5). He stated that as one moves along the continuum, "the degree to which speech is an obligatory accompaniment of gesture decreases from gesticulation to signs" while "the degree to which gesture shows the properties of a language increases" (McNeill, 2008, p. 5).

Furthermore, McNeill (2008) argued that Kendon's continuum is actually a complex combination of multiple continua, each of which is based on a distinct dimension (p. 6). McNeill (2008) went on to describe four of these continua, including the one described previously which maps the relationship of gestures to speech. The three other continua were based on the relationship of gestures to linguistic properties, their relationship to conventions, and finally, their character of semiosis (McNeill, 2008).

In this essay, one gesture taxonomy was described in detail and another was given a brief overview. A distinction was made between interactive and representational gestures, and the latter category was explored in more depth by distinguishing four subcategories within it: deictic, iconic, metaphoric, and emblematic gestures. Kendon's continuum and McNeill's (2008) multiple continua were then introduced as another way to classify gestures and understand them in relation to one another.

There are, naturally, other classifications and countless more examples of gestures used in social interaction that have not been mentioned in this essay. However, although taxonomies differ, the core features of gesture are generally agreed upon. According to McNeill (2008), these core features are the following:

  1. Gestures carry meaning
  2. Gestures and the synchronous speech are co-expressive but not redundant (p. 22)

In other words, gestures and speech both “express the same underlying idea unit but express it in their own ways” (McNeill, 2008, p. 22). One can go even further and posit that synchronised speech and gesture events make up more or less unbreakable units. To support this claim, there is evidence that, for example, delayed auditory feedback does not interrupt the synchrony, that blind speakers gesture to blind interlocutors, and that gesture number and complexity vary with cognitive fluency (McNeill, 2008, p. 24).

The aim of this essay has been to show that gestures have more complexity to them than one might expect. As one of the key premises of multimodality states, "if we want to study meaning, we need to attend to all semiotic resources being used to make a complete whole" (Jewitt et al., 2016, p. 3). Gestures are one of these semiotic resources and it is clear that they do play an important role in the making of multimodal wholes.
